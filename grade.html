## Dopamine Rerouting in Innerdance

Based on the corpus, dopamine rerouting refers to shifting where in the brain dopamine is directed, with significant consequences for experience and awareness.

### The Key Pathways

Dopamine has multiple pathways it can travel to [3]:
- The **prefrontal cortex** (associated with control and regulation)
- The **mesolimbic pathway** (emotional centers, including the amygdala — the "wanting" dopamine)
- The **basal ganglia/striatum** (the reptilian/old brain — the most instinctive brain)

### The Rerouting Mechanism

The critical shift in innerdance involves routing dopamine *away* from the controlling prefrontal cortex and *into* the basal ganglia (old brain). As explained in the corpus:

> "Shifting from number one to number two and three is a routing of dopamine into the old brain. Because when you create a change from slow and restive to something intense, the basal ganglia becomes surprised." [5]

This rerouting is triggered specifically by **surprise and broken expectations** [3][5]. When this happens, dopamine amplifies **glutamate** — the brain's most excitatory neurotransmitter — causing widespread cortical activation: *"the whole cortex just lights up"* [5].

### The Result

When dopamine floods the basal ganglia, it produces the characteristic innerdance movements and altered states [3], essentially bypassing learned patterns of control and creating the conditions for deep transformation [1].

## Epilepsy and Spiritual Experience: Convergent Perspectives

Both sources draw a strong connection between epileptic brain activity and spiritual experience, though they approach it from different angles.

### The Neurological Perspective
The neurology book (*The Spiritual Doorway in the Brain*) notes that seizures have been interpreted as both "demonic and divine" across countless millennia, citing biblical accounts of possession that clearly described seizures [2]. It identifies that **limbic seizures** — stemming from abnormal electrical activity in the temporal and frontal lobes — "can lead to brief but intense spiritual experiences," and suggests that historically significant spiritual figures like Saint Paul, Joan of Arc, and Emanuel Swedenborg may have had seizures as part of their spirituality [2].

### The Innerdance Perspective
The innerdance sessions go further, explicitly framing epilepsy as **"the sacred disease"** and describing it as "spontaneous and sudden spreading of activation of firing patterns across the brain" [4]. Notably, innerdance is described as a *controlled* version of this process — deliberately **lowering threshold potentials** to allow people to "unleash all of this trapped power" [1], while distinguished from clinical seizures partly by its duration, which can last hours rather than minutes [1].

### The Key Convergence
Both sources agree that this neurological firing pattern produces **genuine spiritual experiences** — visions, altered states, and profound encounters — and that history has unfairly pathologized what may be a source of extraordinary human capacity [5].

A central concept is what the materials call **REM intrusion** — the bleeding of REM consciousness into waking states. This occurs naturally in healthy people (roughly a quarter of those studied), and produces phenomena including paralysis, vivid imagery, and hallucinations [1]. ## What Dopamine Is and Isn't
Dopamine is often misunderstood as the "happiness chemical," but it is more accurately described as being about **wanting** to be happy, not happiness itself. There is "very little satisfaction in dopamine as opposed to serotonin" — serotonin lets you *have* something, while dopamine makes you *want* it [3].

---

## How Dopamine is Activated
Dopamine is activated under specific conditions related to **prediction and reward**:
- **No expectation + reward = strong hit** (the "beautiful hit," especially in babies and children learning)
- **Expectation + reward = addictive dopamine** (the "wanting dopamine")
- **Expectation + no reward = no activation**

This is called **error prediction**. Dopamine helps you remember positive experiences so you keep seeking them [1].

---

## Dopamine Pathways
Several distinct pathways are described:

- **Nigrostriatal pathway** (basal ganglia): The "old brain" activation pathway. When dopamine routes here, it amplifies **glutamate** — the most excitatory neurotransmitter — lighting up the entire cortex. This is what produces kundalini-like activation experiences [5].
- **Mesocortical/mesolimbic pathway**: Associated with addictive, controlling dopamine — the "wanting" dopamine tied to emotion and desire [1].
- **Tuberoinfundibular pathway**: Here dopamine acts as **prolactin-inhibiting hormone (PIH)** [3].

---

## Dopamine and Learning
Dopamine plays a foundational role in learning:
- It helps neurons communicate and make predictions about what is good or bad [4].
- Low dopamine leads to inability to concentrate, instability, and inability to learn [4].
- However, **too much dopamine antagonizes glutamate-GABA systems**, which are central to learning — meaning excessive dopamine activation can paradoxically make people "stupid" [3].

---

## Dopamine and Myelination
One of the more significant warnings in the corpus concerns dopamine's inhibition of **prolactin**, which is responsible for producing myelin — the protective membrane that strengthens neural synapses and speeds transmission. **Myelination is described as responsible for genius.** Excessive dopamine suppresses prolactin and thus myelination, with MS cited as a condition related to myelination disruption [3].

---

## Dopamine and Psychosis
Dopamine released via music (in innerdance contexts) can inhibit prolactin through the tuberoinfundibular pathway, potentially triggering **spiritual psychosis** — being present in multiple dimensions of time simultaneously. Antipsychotic medications work precisely by inhibiting dopamine in this pathway. Facilitators who are themselves **dopamine addicted** are more likely to see psychotic episodes in their spaces [2].

---

## Dopamine and the Innerdance Wave
The shift from slow/restive music to intense music **surprises the basal ganglia**, rerouting dopamine from the prefrontal cortex back to the old brain — activating the nigrostriatal pathway and lighting up the whole cortex. This is the neurological basis for the innerdance activation experience [5].
